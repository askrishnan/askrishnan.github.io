Blog #5

I am really enjoying the parts of class where we get to learn about what the young people from HSTF, Sisters Unchained, and (eventually) the Urbano Project have to say about where they are from. The way people represent home is something I've always found very interesting. What is it about where you are from that makes you feel attached to it? Combining my own experience with what I've been reading over the last few weeks, I've figured that family, food, and having people who look like you to look up to are all components of feeling safe in a community. It's reassuring to see so many responses focused on having people who look and grew up like you represented in your community. I want to be able to represent the young people's lives in a way that is appealing to them and their peers, and then the people we're creating for at ISGM.

Quite a few of the girls involved with Sisters Unchained seem like they are poetically inclined (this is based off of the girls in the first video we watched and Janasia's response). Incorperating some kind of poetry with the mapping of their experiences could be really interesting. Since they also already seem to have so much video content, it could also be interesting to utilize that, though I have no idea how. Some kind of patchwork/quilt could be really interesting to put together, but I would want to be cautious about the implications behind it. I remember learning in elementary school that quilts played a large part in passing messages along the route of the Underground Railroad. This narrative has been disputed, so I would like to do more research on it, and make sure that following through with this idea would be respectful and cool (I'm not African American, so I can't decide that). If I were to pursue this, I'd want to align the stories of the girls in Sisters Unchained with some sort of quilt that maps their/their family's journey in relation to incarceration, and ties their stories together. I am honestly a bit nervous that I won't be able to do justice to the stories that the girls from Sisters Unchained are telling us, since the things they face are so far away from what I face. Me hearing their stories and feeling upset and angry for them is very different from what they and their families are actually experiencing, and I want to do their stories justice.

The way the people who filled out the surveys from the HSTF talked about food made me hungry. Food is a huge component of every culture, and it would be really interesting to map people's experiences, at least partially, through food. This could happen in various forms. For example, working with the local businesses, doing a profile on each owner/learning about the origins of a signature dish. Creating a recipe for the area and then showing the origins of each "ingredient" (component of life). A recipe is basically a map for food, after all. It could also be interesting to show how certain traditional foods have been modified to fit American tastes (this could be used to send a message about gentrification). I would also love to be able to incorporate how strongly the young people from the HSTF are against genrification (ex. all of the anti-Whole Foods answers). Showing the different ways a community could evolve over time (ex. the effects of gentrification vs. a thriving community built by immigrants, both extrapolated 10 years in the future, could be interesting to put together with the young people of the HSTF).

Both Sisters Unchained and the HSTF have a strong emphasis on family, and I am still thinking about how to further bring family into this project. Perhaps a description of the same area (or a drawing of a map) by 3 different age brackets? I am still thinking about this one.
