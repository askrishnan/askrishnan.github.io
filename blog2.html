“Sorry We Have No Imagery Here”

I found it almost impossible to wrap my head around how some stuff on Google Maps is censored. Each time I began thinking about it, I would then think, “Yeah, it must be censored, but you’d still be able to tell what is going on from looking at the landscape”, which is untrue! My (and likely most others’) inherent trust in maps makes it difficult to consider that I am only seeing what Google wants to show to me.


This makes me wonder about how governments and other powerful entities decide how much the people need to know. I often hear people throwing around the word “transparency” as one of the most important components of good leadership, but how is it decided what needs to be hidden?


I also noticed that a lot of the more aggressive censoring in the example article is now gone from Google Maps.


Seeing Like a State

When reading the first few pages, I was reminded of a thought I had earlier this week: “What would it be like to see the statistics of my whole life?” For example, the number of times I helped someone or how many liters of water I drank per week on average. That’s just a random thought I wanted to record here (I realized after reading the rest of the article that this thought isn’t that relevant to cadastral mapping).


The section about how the Germans were optimizing the forests was fascinating to me, as we are still learning that nature is very complex to replicate artificially. The idea of “forest hygiene” reminded of how in some parts of the world today there are very few bees, and places are creating an artificial bee to do the real bees’ old job of pollination (21). Something as seemingly tiny as bees has a huge impact on the ecosystem. When they utilized the Norway spruce as a “restoration crop”, the government was thinking primarily of how to make money off of usable wood, not realizing (or caring) about how the locals lived off of the land.


Simplifying data excessively can be dangerous. When planting the “restoration crops” the government dangerously oversimplified what the forest is used for. If there isn’t equal representation in the places where the data is being collected, then important data will be overlooked. In the best case scenario, overlooking important data would be due to ignorance, but in the worst case, it could be attached to power-plays and oppression of others.


Many of the earlier examples in the reading and current examples are tied in with instant gratification. Just because something doesn’t seem efficient or useful in the short term doesn’t mean that it isn’t in the long term. This reminds me of the way a many people think about art vs. engineering. Engineering is a more stable and serious career path, while art often isn’t seen as as lucrative. However, if we devalue art and people stop making it, there will be a large dearth in what people do for enjoyment.
