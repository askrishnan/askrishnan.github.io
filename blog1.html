Borges' "story" presented accuracy from an interesting viewpoint that I don't usually see myself. Generally, when I think of information, I think it is the most valuable when it most closely reflects reality. I personally spend a lot of time trying to perfect things down to the detail, and add components that may not actually be helpful in getting across my point. In fact, the additional information may actually be distracting. Creating a map that is exactly like the real world is pointless because at that point, you can just look at the real world. There are no hot takes or interesting conclusions in a perfectly created map, and it doesn't make it any easier to draw conclusions from the real world. Maps in their origin, are designed to condense information. Even if creating a map of the world exactly as it is were helpful, I doubt that people would actually be able to see the big picture.


Baudrillard's excerpt says that if Borges' excerpt reflected today, the map would be laid out very elaborately, but reality would not reflect what is mapped out. This shows how subjective a lot of information can be when it comes into contact with human descision making. Sometimes, an ideal version of reality is shown in record keeping, or the time spent record-keeping takes attention away from the territory that is real. The territory could be evolving as we rush to record information about it. Additionally, most recorded information is subject to some sort of bias by whoever is recording it, no matter how objective they think they are.


Paglen's paper made me think more about the previous ideas of trying to map everything. Believing that the content of what is already mapped is the absolute truth can be harmful to progress. His explanation using the university was interesting to me--it made me wonder about how we can capture both obviously material things like buildings, and experiences, like attending class and the operations needed to keep the school running. How can we show all of the social and cultural steps that build up into the creation of art?

I ended up having 3 questions/takeaways from reading the 3 articles together:

  • How do we know when we have mapped enough/too much?
  • How do we know when it is time to question what we have mapped instead of following it just because it exists?
  • How do we show the space in which the map was created, and why it is designed the way it is?
