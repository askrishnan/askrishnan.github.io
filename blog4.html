Sisters Unchained

Watching the Sisters Unchained video drove home how little support there is for the relatives of people who are incarcerated. Many of the interviews referenced how rarely the girls see their relatives who are incarcerated, to the point where Loyalty and Unity barely knew who their dad was when he got out of prison. Many of the girls are raised by other members of their families. Sometimes this requires them to uproot their entire lives to move with other parts of the family. Kyndia mentions having to grow up by herself, living with her grandmother, and having to figure out the parts of her schooling on her own. Several of the girls were very close to their incarcerated family members, and going from always being with them to never seeing them seems like an impossibly difficult transition.

Major takeaways

  • The girls rarely get to see their incarcerated relatives (if their relative was taken to prison when they were very young, they generally don't have a strong relationship with them; it is more of a story they are told)
  • Their entire lives are uprooted if a primary member of their support system is incarcerated.
  • If they do not have a close relationship/resources in the new living situation, the girls have to figure out a lot on their own.

HSTF

The youth notes from the HSTF made it clear that many of the young people are interested in creating an uncensored space just for them. They are proud of the Latinx representation in the area, but hesitant about the potential gentrification that is encroaching on the Latin Quarter. The young people love the events held on the street, and would like more block parties, etc. I think that these events help condense many of the good vibes of the Latin Quarter into one fun event for people to attend. They want to create a larger sense of community by sprucing up HSTF and making it brigher.

The current map of the Latin Quarter conveys a lot of valueable information, but it isn't really presented in a tantalizing way. Showing examples of whant the small businesses sell and what all of the resources look like could be really valueable. Making the colors brighter, like the suggestions in the youth notes, and making the style of the map more eye catching could make the map a more persuasive suggestion to go to the Latin Quarter. If I were to redesign this, I would center the map, keep the descriptions of the local business close to their names, and show pictures somewhere on the visual (perhaps over the map). Even drawing the storefronts could add a certain amount of appeal to the map. This map offers a lot of important information and condenses it well, but it also has a great opportunities to create a vibe for the Latin Quarter before people even get there (though I am not sure this should be the first goal, since I don't know much about the correlation between marketing in area and increase in gentrification).


Part 2


The Social Life of Urban Spaces was fascinating to watch. I can't believe that a group of people really put so much time into researching the use of various public spaces. Some of the metrics that they were analyzing were so minute that I couldn't believe they had thought to analyze them, but they were also so telling. It seems that many public spaces are not really designed for people, or at least for what real people do in them. It's important that we don't assume that we know stuff about our stakeholders, and instead back up our findings with fact (ex. the use of fixed seats when people prefer sitting in movable ones, or how many people go to public spaces and people watch).

Victoria Okoye's blog posts pointed at how sometimes, the use of certain techniques in planning can reinforce outdated plans. Generally, those with dominant identities get their views strongly reflected in the rules, while the people living in the margins have to adapt to live their lives. To avoid reproducing marginalization, it is important to have representation from various groups, and to look at what people are doing in a makeshift way (ex. the generator phone chargers or improvising due to lack of running water). Even looking at these makeshift solutions can tell a lot about how we can change current rules and practices to help more than just the dominany population.

The C3's approach to counter mapping (doing it autonomously), attempts to change the fact that those in power have many opportunities to manipulate data. C3 is working with several groups, just like we are, and they did deeper reseach into how UNC-CH currently runs before they began mapping. The realization that the way people live and act should have a large effect on what is being mapped is an important one. C3 made valueable insights about the workstyles of UNC-CH people (ex. the line between work and leisure is very blurred, creating a chance to have people working even during what they think is downtime). Research that is perpetuating only one viewpoint can be a militant action, and countering it with research that takes into account many viewpoints can be very powerful.
